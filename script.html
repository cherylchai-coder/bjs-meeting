Part One

The Conviction Arc (Slides 0–5)

00
Title — "The Vision for AI Concierge"
~2 min Warm, grounded, conversational

"Thanks for having us. Before we get into anything, I want to share how we think about what we're building — not just the product, but the philosophy behind it."

Don't oversell. Don't pitch. You're setting the tone: this is a conversation between partners, not a vendor demo. Make eye contact with Scott.

"This isn't a feature walkthrough. It's a point of view on where AI concierge is going, built specifically around your members and what Bev can become."

Talking Points
  • Acknowledge the team by name — Scott, Adam, Anirudh. Make it personal.
  • Frame the next 45 minutes: conviction first, then product, then discussion.
  • Signal you're here to listen, not just present. "We have a strong point of view, but we want to pressure-test it with you."
Don't Say
  • "We're excited to show you..." — too salesy.
  • Don't start with company stats or a history lesson about Sendbird.
→ Click to Slide 1. No verbal bridge needed — the slide itself is a bold statement. Let it appear, then speak into it.
01
"An AI that remembers your members is an AI that drives revenue."
~3 min Confident, insight-driven — make eye contact

"You've already seen what Bev can do with recommendations and coupons. But here's what we've learned working across retail, travel, food delivery — the AI that remembers is the AI that sells."

"Memory compounds. Every interaction Bev has — every coupon Sarah clips, every product Marcus searches for — it gets smarter. Not session by session. Over the relationship."

Humor"Your members don't want to be treated like strangers every time they open the app. That's like going to your favorite restaurant and the waiter asks 'have you been here before?' for the 50th time."

Talking Points
  • Memory isn't a feature — it's the foundation. Without it, every conversation starts from zero.
  • This is the compounding flywheel: more interactions = better memory = more relevant = more interactions.
  • Bev already has the beginnings of this with shopping. The opportunity is to extend it everywhere.
  • If they push back: "We've seen this pattern. Retailers who add memory see 20-40% higher engagement within 90 days."
→ "And that's especially true for you. Because you're not just a retailer." Click to Slide 2.
02
"Membership is a relationship."
~3 min Direct, conviction — this is YOUR thesis about BJ's

"Here's what hit us when we started thinking about BJ's specifically. You're not a retailer. You're a membership club. People PAY to be here. That changes everything."

"When someone pays $60 or $120 a year, they're not just a customer. They've opted in. They want a relationship. And they have expectations."

Slow down here. This is a conviction beat. Let it breathe.

"Think about it — Bev should know when Sarah's kid is heading to college. She's been buying dorm stuff, her basket changed. That's not a transaction. That's a life moment. And Bev can show up for it."

"The 2% cash back isn't why your best members renew. They renew because they feel like BJ's knows them."

Pause

Talking Points
  • 8 million members. Club+ is $120/year for 2% cash back. But the real retention driver is emotional, not financial.
  • Life moment detection: college move, new baby, holiday hosting. Bev can infer these from purchase patterns.
  • This is where BJ's has an advantage over Amazon — you have the membership relationship. Amazon has transactions.
Don't Say
  • "Loyalty" as a buzzword. Be specific — say "renewal" or "relationship."
  • Don't name-drop Costco. BJ's knows who they compete with.
→ "So Bev remembers. That's half the equation. But what happens when something goes wrong?" Click to Slide 3.
03
"Your members also need an AI that fights for them."
~3 min Provocative — plant the seed for Steward

"So Bev remembers. Great. But what happens when the curbside order shows up missing three items?"

Let that hang for a beat.

"The industry calls 50% automation a success. We call that 50% giving up. Half the time, a member with a real problem gets handed off to a human who starts from scratch."

"That's not good enough for members who are paying to be here."

Talking Points
  • The 50% handoff stat is real and it stings. Most AI handles FAQ and order status, then punts everything else.
  • Plant the seed: there's a second capability coming. Don't name Steward yet. Build the tension.
  • The emotional angle: members who have a bad support experience are 4x more likely to not renew. Even if the issue gets resolved.
→ "Let me put a finer point on this." Click to Slide 4.
04
"Support shouldn't be the worst part of membership."
~2 min Let it land. This slide does the work.

"Think about it. Your members chose you. They're paying $60 to $120 a year. And when they need help, the experience is... hold music."

Pause — let the room feel this one. Don't rush. 3-4 seconds of silence.

"We can fix that."

Talking Points
  • This is the emotional peak of the conviction arc. Less is more. Don't over-explain.
  • If someone nods or reacts — good. Acknowledge it. "You've felt that, right?"
  • BJ's hasn't launched member care yet. This slide is validating a problem they already know they have.
→ "Here's how." Click to Slide 5 — the thesis statement.
05
"AI that remembers + AI that acts"
~2 min Punchy thesis. Framework slide. Quick.

"So that's our thesis. Two capabilities. AI that remembers — that's FYC, For You Conversations. AI that acts — that's Agent Steward. Everything else is built on top of these two."

Quick and punchy. This is the thesis statement. Don't overexplain. Let the framework click.

"FYC is what makes Bev personal. Steward is what makes Bev powerful. You need both."

Talking Points
  • This is the anchor. Everything before was building to it. Everything after proves it.
  • FYC = For You Conversations. Personalization layer. Already partially live with Bev shopping.
  • Agent Steward = the action layer. Investigates, coordinates, resolves. New capability.
  • Don't linger. Move to the proof.
→ "Let me show you why we believe this, and where the market is." Click to Slide 6.
Part Two

Market Context & Framework (Slides 6–7)

06
Three Waves of AI
~3 min Context-setting. Brisk through 1 & 2, land on 3.

"Quick market context. We work with companies across retail, travel, food delivery, healthcare. The pattern is the same everywhere."

Walk through Wave 1 and Wave 2 quickly — 15 seconds each, max. They're context, not the point.

"Wave 1 was chatbots. Scripted, limited, frustrating. Wave 2 is where most companies are now — LLM-powered, can answer questions, feels smarter, but still reactive. Still forgets you."

"Wave 3 is where we are. Proactive. Memory-powered. Can contact external parties. Earns autonomy through data. This is what Bev becomes."

Talking Points
  • Wave 3 keywords: proactive, persistent memory, cross-party coordination, earned autonomy.
  • Most competitors are stuck in Wave 2. BJ's opportunity is to skip ahead.
  • If they ask "where are we now?" — "Bev shopping is early Wave 2. We're taking you to Wave 3."
→ "Wave 3 needs four specific things." Click to Slide 7.
07
Four Capabilities
~2 min Framework. Quick tour, then into the product.

"Wave 3 needs four things. Memory, proactivity, async action, and earned autonomy."

Point to the visual. Two boxes are FYC, two are Steward.

"Top two — memory and proactivity — that's FYC territory. Bottom two — async action and earned autonomy — that's Steward."

"Let me show you what these look like in practice. Starting with what Bev already does."

Talking Points
  • Memory = persistent understanding of each member.
  • Proactivity = Bev reaching out first, not waiting to be asked.
  • Async action = coordinating across systems and parties in the background.
  • Earned autonomy = AI proves it can handle more, based on accuracy data.
→ Click to Slide 8 — FYC section break.

For You Conversations — The Remembering Part (Slides 8–10)

08
FYC Section Break

"Section one — For You Conversations. The concierge that knows you."

Quick breath. Let the section break visual register. Then click through.

09
Bev Shopping Chat UI — Three Members
~4 min Demo-style. Walk through each persona.

"Three different members, three different greetings. Sarah gets a coupon reminder because Bev knows she bought Bounty last month. Marcus gets a Super Bowl nudge because Bev remembers last year. Priya gets a back-in-stock alert on the Ninja Creami she asked about."

Point to the memory labels on screen as you talk.

"See these tags? Past purchase, buy frequency, seasonal pattern, wishlist. That's what's powering each message."

"This isn't generic. This is personal. At scale."

Humor"Nobody else's AI remembers that Marcus throws a Super Bowl party every year. That's the difference."

Talking Points
  • Sarah = the coupon clipper. Club+ member. Past-purchase memory + active coupon matching.
  • Marcus = the seasonal buyer. Bev remembers his Super Bowl routine from last year.
  • Priya = the wishlist watcher. Asked about Ninja Creami weeks ago. Bev follows up when it's back.
  • The memory tags are the key visual — make sure they point to them. These are proof of what's driving personalization.
  • If they ask: "Yes, this works with your existing product catalog and coupon APIs."
Don't Say
  • Don't call these "personas" — they're members. Real people.
  • Don't get into the technical weeds of how memory storage works unless asked.
→ "Those are three moments. Let me zoom out and show you the full picture." Click to Slide 10.
10
Member Lifecycle — 10 Use Cases
~3 min Strategic. Don't read every item. Hit highlights.

"Here's the full picture — 10 use cases across the lifecycle. Before they shop, while they shop, after they buy."

Don't read every item. Sweep your hand across the visual, then zero in on two or three.

"The quick wins are cart abandonment and order status — those are day one. Strategic bets like replenishment and seasonal are where memory really compounds."

"This is a continuous loop. The post-purchase experience feeds back into the next shopping session."

Talking Points
  • Pre-shop: proactive deals, replenishment reminders, seasonal nudges.
  • During shop: product finder, coupon matching, comparison help.
  • Post-purchase: order tracking, review nudge, return help — which bridges to Steward.
  • Cart abandonment is the easiest quick win. Known ROI, immediate impact.
  • Replenishment is the sleeper hit — Bev knows you buy Bounty every 6 weeks, pings you at week 5.
→ "OK. That's the remembering part. Now let's talk about the acting part." Click to Slide 11.
Part Four

Agent Steward — The Acting Part (Slides 11–15)

11
Steward Section Break / Pivot
~2 min Pivotal moment. Change the energy.

"OK. That's the remembering part. Now let's talk about the acting part."

Shift your energy here. Lean forward slightly. This is the pivot.

"Bev remembers what you love. But who fights for you when the delivery is wrong, the charge doesn't look right, or you're thinking about cancelling?"

Pause — let it land. This is the question that opens the Steward section.

Talking Points
  • This is the emotional hinge of the whole presentation.
  • You're reframing the problem: it's not "support automation" — it's "who has the member's back?"
  • BJ's hasn't launched member care yet. This is your chance to shape how they think about it from day one.
→ "Let me show you the gap." Click to Slide 12.
12
The Gap Diagram
~4 min Diagnostic. Walk through the visual carefully.

"This is what happens today when a case gets complex. AI handles the easy stuff — FAQ, order status. But the moment it needs to call the warehouse, email a vendor, coordinate a return... it hits this gap."

Point to the red barrier in the visual.

"THE GAP. AI hands off. Human takes over. Does all the coordination manually. The member waits."

Now point to the bottom panel.

"With Steward, there is no gap. Bev sits in the middle, coordinates across all parties, and the member gets a resolution — not a transfer."

Watch for the animated lines on screen.

"See those green lines? That's data flowing. Steward is actively talking to every party — the warehouse, the payment system, the member — simultaneously."

Talking Points
  • The top panel = today's reality for most companies. AI does the easy 50%, humans do the hard 50%.
  • The bottom panel = Steward. AI investigates, coordinates, proposes resolution. Human approves.
  • Key insight: the member never gets transferred. One conversation, start to finish.
  • If they ask about integration: "Steward connects via APIs to your order system, warehouse, payment, and any third-party vendor."
Don't Say
  • "Replaces your CS team" — say "augments" or "handles the coordination so your team handles the judgment calls."
  • Don't trash their current support setup. You don't know the details. Be respectful.
→ "Let me show you what this actually looks like in action." Click to Slide 13.
13
Orchestration Animation — Missing Curbside Items
~4 min Let the animation breathe. Narrate lightly.

Let the animation start. Don't talk over it for the first few seconds. Let them watch.

"Missing curbside items. This is the scenario."

Narrate key moments as they appear:

"Sarah reports missing items... Bev checks the order API... calls the warehouse... updates Sarah... sends a summary to the CS agent for approval... and resolves it."

"One conversation. Zero handoffs. The member never had to call anyone."

Humor"The CS agent's job here? Type '1' to approve. That's it. That's the whole job."

Talking Points
  • The animation shows the full orchestration flow. Let it do the heavy lifting.
  • Key moments: (1) member reports, (2) Bev investigates via API, (3) Bev contacts warehouse, (4) Bev proposes resolution, (5) human approves, (6) member gets resolution.
  • The human-in-the-loop is the approval step. Steward does the work, human gives the green light.
  • "Curbside missing items" was chosen because it's BJ's-specific and high-frequency pain.
Don't Say
  • "Fully automated" — always emphasize the human approval step.
  • Don't narrate every single animation step. Less is more. Let the visual land.
→ "What you just saw? That's Level 1." Click to Slide 14.
14
Bridge — "What you just saw is Level 1"
~1 min Quick bridge. Tease what's next.

"What you just saw? That's Level 1. AI recommends, human approves. But it doesn't stop there."

This is a one-breath slide. Tease the staircase and move on.

→ Click immediately to Slide 15.
15
Autonomy Staircase
~4 min Key framework. Spend time here. This builds trust.

"Level 0 is where most AI is today — handles simple stuff, hands off the rest."

"Level 1 is Steward — investigates, coordinates, proposes a resolution. Human still approves."

"Level 2 is where Steward proves — through accuracy data — that it can go end-to-end. Each use case earns autonomy independently."

Pause

"We don't ask you to trust the AI. The AI earns the trust."

Talking Points
  • This is the trust-building slide. Linger here. It addresses the #1 objection: "How do we know the AI won't mess up?"
  • Each use case earns autonomy INDEPENDENTLY. Refund for missing items might reach L2 while warranty claims stay at L1.
  • "Earned" is the key word. Autonomy is based on data — accuracy rates, resolution times, member satisfaction.
  • Analogy if needed: "It's like a new employee. You check every decision at first. Then you spot-check. Then you trust them with more."
  • If they ask about timeline: "L1 is where you start. L2 is typically 3-6 months of proven accuracy on a specific use case."
Don't Say
  • "Fully autonomous" without the "earned through data" qualifier. ALWAYS pair autonomy with proof.
  • "Replaces agents" — say "frees agents to handle the cases that need human judgment."
  • "Trust us" — the whole point is that the DATA earns trust, not your promise.
→ "Now here's why this works as one platform, not two separate tools." Click to Slide 16.
Part Five

Platform & Roadmap (Slides 16–17)

16
Platform Flywheel
~3 min Connecting the dots. Flywheel energy.

"Here's why this all works as one platform. Shopping builds memory. Memory powers personalization. When issues come up, the same memory powers resolution. Resolution builds trust. Trust drives renewal."

"It's a flywheel. Every interaction makes the next one better."

"And both — shopping and care — are on the same platform. Shared context. When Bev resolves a delivery issue, she remembers it next time the member shops."

Talking Points
  • This is the "why one platform" argument. Shopping and care on separate systems = fragmented member experience.
  • Example: Sarah had a missing-item issue last month. Next time she shops, Bev can say "Hey, want me to double-check your curbside order this time?"
  • The flywheel: shop → memory → personalization → issue → resolution → trust → renewal → shop again.
  • Competitive moat: the longer you're on one platform, the richer the memory, the harder it is to replicate.
→ "So how do we get there? Here's the roadmap." Click to Slide 17.
17
Roadmap — Two Parallel Tracks
~3 min Practical. Forward-looking. Set up the closing convo.

"Two parallel tracks. Left side: Bev and FYC — that's already in motion. Right side: Member Care and Steward — this is the new muscle."

"You don't need to launch Steward before Member Care. You launch Member Care, get baseline data, then Steward takes over the complex cases."

"The stairs go up because each phase builds on the previous one. Quick wins, then strategic bets, then memory compounds. Launch care, then Steward L1, then earned autonomy."

Talking Points
  • Left track (FYC): enhance Bev shopping → add memory layers → proactive FYC → full lifecycle coverage.
  • Right track (Steward): launch member care → get baseline metrics → Steward L1 on top cases → earn L2 on proven use cases.
  • The tracks are parallel but connected — same platform, shared memory.
  • Be honest: "Member Care launch is the foundation. You need the data before Steward can add value."
  • If they ask timing: be specific about what you can commit to, and honest about what requires their input.
Don't Say
  • Don't promise specific dates unless you've confirmed with the team.
  • Don't oversell Steward as "ready to go" — be honest about the rollout phases.
→ Don't click to a new slide. Close the laptop lid or go to a blank screen. End with a conversation, not a slide.

Closing — Open Discussion (~5+ min)

"That's the vision. Two capabilities — remembers and acts — on one platform. We'd love to talk about which FYC use cases to prioritize, what the highest-pain member care cases look like, and whether we can set some dates."

Don't end with a slide. End face-to-face. Close the deck.

Closing Talking Points
  • Ask, don't tell: "What resonates? What are we missing?"
  • If they like FYC: "Which use cases matter most? Cart abandonment? Replenishment? Seasonal?"
  • If they like Steward: "What are the top 3 member care cases by volume? Missing items? Returns? Billing?"
  • Push for next steps: "Can we get a working session on the calendar to map out the member care use cases?"
  • Leave-behind: the deck is at bjs-meeting.vercel.app (pw: bjs). They can revisit anytime.
Don't Say
  • "Any questions?" — instead say "What resonates? What are we missing?"
  • Don't end with a recap of everything you just said. They were there.
  • Don't pitch pricing. If they ask, say "Let's figure out scope first, then we'll build a proposal."

General Rules — Keep These in Your Head

Always Do

  • "Members" not "customers"
  • "Club+" not "premium tier"
  • Reference their products: Bounty, Ninja Creami, Kirkland
  • Acknowledge what Bev already does before introducing new
  • Be honest about what's new vs. proven
  • Let animations and visuals do the work
  • Make eye contact on conviction slides (1–5)
  • Use names — Scott, Adam, Anirudh
  • Pair "autonomy" with "earned through data"

Never Say

  • "Synergy" or "leverage"
  • "At the end of the day"
  • "Game-changer" or "disruptive"
  • "Circle back" or "take offline"
  • "Fully autonomous" without the earned qualifier
  • "Any questions?" (say "What resonates?")
  • Competitor names
  • Overpromise on unbuilt features
  • "Customers" (it's "members")
