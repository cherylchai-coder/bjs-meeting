Title — "The Vision for AI Concierge"
~2 min Warm, grounded, conversational

"Thanks for having us. Before we get into anything, I want to share how we think about what we're building — not just the product, but the philosophy behind it."

Don't oversell. Don't pitch. You're setting the tone: this is a conversation between partners, not a vendor demo. Make eye contact with Scott.

"Here's the thing — every AI company right now has memory, agents, automation. The technology is converging. If you line up the feature lists, honestly, a lot of products look the same."

"So what's the moat? It's not the features. It's the opinions behind the features. What you believe about how AI should work. How fast you ship. And the product decisions that accumulate over time into something nobody else has."

"These opinions didn't come from a whiteboard. They came from deploying AI concierges across retail, travel, food delivery, healthcare — working with companies like Norse Atlantic, Coupang, and others who are live in production right now. The patterns we're going to share with you today are things we've learned the hard way, across industries, at scale."

This is your credibility moment. You're not theorizing — you've seen what works and what doesn't across multiple verticals. Don't name-drop excessively, but make it clear these are earned insights, not marketing.

"So today isn't a feature list. It's a point of view — shaped by real deployments — and we want to pressure-test it with you."

Talking Points
  • Acknowledge the team by name — Scott, Adam, Anirudh. Make it personal.
  • Frame the next 45 minutes: conviction first, then product, then discussion.
  • Credibility anchors you can drop naturally: "We power 7 billion+ conversations a month across our platform." / "Norse Atlantic is live with Steward right now for refund cases." / "We've seen the 50% handoff problem firsthand at Coupang — that's where the Steward idea came from."
  • Signal you're here to listen, not just present. "We have a strong point of view, but we want to hear where it lands for you."
Don't Say
  • "We're excited to show you..." — too salesy.
  • Don't start with company stats or a history lesson about Sendbird.

"An AI that remembers your members is an AI that drives revenue."
~3 min Confident, insight-driven — make eye contact

"You've already seen what Bev can do with recommendations and coupons. But here's what we've learned working across retail, travel, food delivery — the AI that remembers is the AI that sells."

"Memory compounds. Every interaction Bev has, she gets smarter. Not session by session — over the relationship."

"Here's what that looks like at BJ's. Sarah clips coupons for cleaning supplies every month. Bev learns that pattern. Three months in, Bev knows Sarah's a Bounty buyer who shops bulk cleaning on Saturdays, prefers the Framingham club, and has three kids. Now Bev isn't just surfacing random deals — she's catching the $4 off Bounty coupon before it expires, nudging the back-to-school supplies in August, and reminding Sarah her Club+ membership renews in two weeks with $847 in savings."

"Marcus searches for party supplies every January. First time, Bev just shows results. Second year, Bev says 'hey, Super Bowl's coming up — want to reorder what you got last year?' Third year, Bev proactively sends him a message before he even opens the app."

"That's the compound effect. Month one, Bev is helpful. Month six, she's indispensable. By year two, your members feel like Bev actually knows them."

Humor"Your members don't want to be treated like strangers every time they open the app. That's like going to your favorite restaurant and the waiter asks 'have you been here before?' for the 50th time."

Talking Points
  • Memory isn't a feature — it's the foundation. Without it, every conversation starts from zero.
  • This is the compounding flywheel: more interactions = better memory = more relevant = more interactions.
  • Bev already has the beginnings of this with shopping. The opportunity is to extend it everywhere.
  • If they push back: "We've seen this pattern. Retailers who add memory see 20-40% higher engagement within 90 days."

02
"Membership is a relationship."
~3 min Direct, conviction — this is YOUR thesis about BJ's

"Here's what hit us when we started thinking about BJ's specifically. You're not a retailer. You're a membership club. People PAY to be here. That changes everything."

"Think about your clubs. When a member walks into the Framingham store, the experience is great. The staff recognizes regulars. Someone in the bakery knows Mrs. Chen always gets the croissants. The tire guy remembers Marcus brought his truck in last spring. That's the in-store experience. You control it. It's personal."

"But here's the problem. That's maybe 15% of your interactions. The other 85% — app, website, curbside, delivery — the member is basically anonymous. No one remembers them. No one knows their history. They start from scratch every time."

This is the 15% vs 85% framing from the cult brand pitch. Let it land. They'll feel this.

"Now — remembering 10 members is easy. Your store team does it naturally. But how do you remember 8 million? How do you make every single one of them feel like the bakery knows their name — at scale, across every channel, every visit?"

"That's what Bev is. She's the store associate who never forgets. She knows Sarah's kid is heading to college because the basket changed. She knows Marcus is a Super Bowl guy. She knows Priya's been eyeing the Ninja Creami. Except she does it for 8 million members simultaneously."

"The 2% cash back isn't why your best members renew. They renew because they feel like BJ's knows them. Bev makes that scale."

Pause

Talking Points
  • The 15% vs 85% gap: In-store is personal. Digital is anonymous. Bev closes that gap.
  • 8 million members. Remembering 10 is easy. Remembering 8 million requires AI with persistent memory.
  • Life moment detection: college move, new baby, holiday hosting — Bev infers these from purchase pattern changes.
  • This is where BJ's has an advantage over Amazon — you have the membership relationship. Amazon has transactions. But only if the digital experience matches the in-store warmth.

"AI that remembers + AI that acts"
~2 min Punchy thesis. Framework slide. Quick.

"So that's our thesis. Two capabilities. AI that remembers — that's FYC. AI that acts — that's Agent Steward."

"And here's the thing — these didn't come out of nowhere. Over the past year we've been building the ingredients together. Channels. Personalization. Welcome messages. Omnichannel infrastructure. At the time, those felt like individual features. But they were actually foundations."

"FYC is what happens when you combine memory with personalization with proactive messaging. It's not a new product — it's all those ingredients working together with purpose."

"And Steward? Steward literally only works because we already have omnichannel. How does AI call the warehouse, SMS the delivery partner, email the vendor, and chat with the member — all at the same time? Because those channels already exist. We built them. Now Steward uses them."

This is the "natural evolution" moment. You're showing that this isn't a pivot or a random new feature — it's the logical next step of everything you've been building together.

"Honestly — we're past the point of talking about simple use cases and channels as standalone features. That was last year. This year, it's about what those ingredients become when you put them together. And they become two things: an AI that remembers, and an AI that acts."

Talking Points
  • The evolution story: Channels → personalization → proactive messaging → FYC. Omnichannel → async coordination → Steward. Natural progression, not a leap.
  • BJ's has already been on this journey with you — Bev shopping is the proof that the foundation works.
  • Steward requires omnichannel (voice, SMS, email, chat, desk) — and you already have it. No one else can say that.
  • The "we're past simple use cases" line signals maturity. You're not selling channels anymore. You're selling outcomes.
  • Don't linger too long — make the point, then move to the market context.

15
Autonomy Staircase
~4 min Key framework. Spend time here. This builds trust.

"I want to be really clear about something. We CAN build fully autonomous AI. The capability exists. That's not the hard part."

"The hard part is doing it safely. In a way that's measurable. In a way where you sleep at night knowing AI just issued a $200 credit and it was the right call."

Point to the three levels. Walk up the staircase visually.

"Level 0 is where most AI is today. It handles the simple stuff — FAQ, order status, balance checks. The moment something needs a judgment call or coordination, it hands off. That's the 50% handoff we talked about."

"Level 1 is Steward. This is where it gets interesting. Steward does ALL the work — investigates, contacts the warehouse, gathers evidence, synthesizes everything — and then packages a recommendation for a human. 'I found a shipping error on order #4821. Recommend full replacement. Approve, modify, or reject.' The human's job is one click."

"Now here's the key. While Steward is at L1, we're measuring. Every recommendation Steward makes, we compare against what the human actually decided. Did the human approve as-is? Modify? Reject? We're building an accuracy score, per use case, over hundreds of cases."

"When that accuracy hits 98% — meaning Steward's recommendations match human judgment 98 times out of 100 — you have a choice. You can graduate that specific use case to Level 2. Steward goes end-to-end. No human in the loop."

This is the "earns its own retirement" moment. Lean into it.

"And this happens per use case, independently. Missing curbside items might reach L2 in three months because the pattern is consistent. Warranty claims might stay at L1 longer because they're more complex. Each one earns it on its own timeline, based on its own data."

Pause

"So we're not asking you to flip a switch and trust the AI. We're saying: let Steward prove it. Case by case. Use case by use case. When the data says it's ready, you promote it. When it's not, you keep the human in the loop. Zero risk."

"Steward earns its own retirement from human oversight."

Talking Points
  • Lead with "we CAN do full autonomy" — this isn't a limitation, it's a choice. A safer, measurable choice.
  • L1 is where the data gets built. Every recommendation vs. human decision = accuracy score.
  • 98% accuracy threshold is the graduation bar. They can set their own threshold if they want higher.
  • Per use case, independently. This is critical. They don't have to go all-in. Each case type earns it separately.
  • The Woowa/Coupang learning: "We presented this to a Korean food delivery company. They said 'we want full automation.' We said 'great — Steward gets you there, but with a data trail. In 3 months their simple refund cases were at L2."
  • Analogy: "New employee on probation. You review every decision at first. After 200 perfect calls, you stop checking. That's Steward."
  • If they push back on needing L1 at all: "You could skip to L2 for low-risk cases on day one — like order status updates. Steward L1 is for the cases where money moves."
Don't Say
  • "Fully autonomous" without immediately following with "earned through data." ALWAYS pair them.
  • "Replaces agents" — say "frees agents to handle the cases that genuinely need human judgment."
  • "Trust us" — the whole point is that the DATA earns trust, not your promise.
  • Don't frame L1 as a limitation. Frame it as an investment in confidence. "L1 isn't the ceiling. It's the launchpad."

Part Five

Platform & Roadmap (Slides 16–17)

16
Platform Flywheel
~3 min Connecting the dots. Flywheel energy.

"Here's why this all works as one platform. Shopping builds memory. Memory powers personalization. When issues come up, the same memory powers resolution. Resolution builds trust. Trust drives renewal."

"It's a flywheel. Every interaction makes the next one better."

"And both — shopping and care — are on the same platform. Shared context. When Bev resolves a delivery issue, she remembers it next time the member shops."

Talking Points
  • This is the "why one platform" argument. Shopping and care on separate systems = fragmented member experience.
  • Example: Sarah had a missing-item issue last month. Next time she shops, Bev can say "Hey, want me to double-check your curbside order this time?"
  • The flywheel: shop → memory → personalization → issue → resolution → trust → renewal → shop again.
  • Competitive moat: the longer you're on one platform, the richer the memory, the harder it is to replicate.
→ "So how do we get there? Here's the roadmap." Click to Slide 17.

Roadmap — Two Parallel Tracks
~3 min Practical. Forward-looking. Set up the closing convo.

"Two parallel tracks. Left side: Bev and FYC — that's already in motion. Right side: Member Care and Steward — this is the new muscle."

  • Right track (Steward): launch member care → get baseline metrics → Steward L1 on top cases → earn L2 on proven use cases.
  • The tracks are parallel but connected — same platform, shared memory.
  • Be honest: "Member Care launch is the foundation. You need the data before Steward can add value."
  • If they ask timing: be specific about what you can commit to, and honest about what requires their input.
Don't Say
  • Don't promise specific dates unless you've confirmed with the team.
  • Don't oversell Steward as "ready to go" — be honest about the rollout phases.

General Rules — Keep These in Your Head

Always Do

  • "Members" not "customers"
  • "Club+" not "premium tier"
  • Reference their products: Bounty, Ninja Creami, Kirkland
  • Acknowledge what Bev already does before introducing new
  • Be honest about what's new vs. proven
  • Let animations and visuals do the work
  • Make eye contact on conviction slides (1–5)
  • Use names — Scott, Adam, Anirudh
  • Pair "autonomy" with "earned through data"

Never Say

  • "Synergy" or "leverage"
  • "At the end of the day"
  • "Game-changer" or "disruptive"
  • "Circle back" or "take offline"
  • "Fully autonomous" without the earned qualifier
  • "Any questions?" (say "What resonates?")
  • Competitor names
  • Overpromise on unbuilt features
  • "Customers" (it's "members")
